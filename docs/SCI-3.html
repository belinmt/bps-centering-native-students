Subsection 2.3.1 1st Grade

Context 1: Supplement to “Science through Story: The Sun, Moon, and Stars”.

This context builds off of Lesson Two, “Science through Story: The Sun, Moon, and Stars” in the “Pueblo Creation Story: Our Awe Inspiring Cosmos” unit from the Indigenous Wisdom: Centuries of Pueblo Impact in New Mexico Pueblo-based educational curriculum accessed through the Indian Pueblo Cultural Center website.

Example 2.3.1. Standard 1-ESS1-1.

Description: Use observations of the sun, moon, and stars to describe patterns that can be predicted.

Suggested classroom activities: Conduct Lesson Two, “Science through Story: The Sun, Moon, and Stars”. Ask students to ask family or community members how to describe the sun, moon, and stars in their Native language(s).

Example 2.3.2. Standard 1-ESS2-2.

Description: Make observations at different times of year to relate the amount of daylight to the time of year.

Suggested classroom activities: Refer to the “Pacing Guide for Teachers of Pueblo Students”. Ask students to reflect on the amount of daylight during the community activities listed during different parts of the year.

Example 2.3.3. Standard 1-PS4-2.

Description: Make observations to construct an evidence-based account that objects in darkness can be seen only when illuminated.

Conduct Lesson Two, “Science through Story: The Sun, Moon, and Stars”. Take home exercise: Ask students to go home and observe the stars and to find the constellations from the “Culminating Activity” of “Pueblo Creation Story: Our Awe Inspiring Cosmos”. Ask students to ask their family or community members if there are names in their Native language(s) to the different constellations. Questions: What time was it easiest for you to see the constellations? How “bright” was the moon? Why do you think the moon “brighter” at night? If the moon was bright, did you see more stars closer or further away from the moon? Why do you think it is easier to see stars at night?

Example 2.3.4. Standard 1-PS4-3.

Description: Plan and conduct investigations to determine the effect of placing objects made with different materials in the path of a beam of light.

Take home exercise: When going outside to observe the constellations (from take home activity in 1-PS4-2), ask students to take a flashlight and shine it on different objects outside.

Context 2: Three sisters.

Describe to students the concept of the “Three sisters” where corns, beans, and squash are planted together to help each other grow. (https://www.nativeseeds.org/blogs/blog-news/how-to-grow-a-three-sisters-garden). 27 

Example 2.3.5. Standard 1-LS1-1.

Description: Use materials to design a solution to a human problem by mimicking how plants and/or animals use their external parts to help them survive, grow, and meet their needs.

Suggested classroom activities: Describe to students how “Corn provides tall stalks for the beans to climb so that they are not out-competed by sprawling squash vines. Beans provide nitrogen to fertilize the soil while also stabilizing the tall corn during heavy winds. Beans are nitrogen-fixers meaning they host rhizobia on their roots that can take nitrogen, a much needed plant nutrient, from the air and convert it into forms that can be absorbed by plant roots. The large leaves of squash plants shade the ground which helps retain soil moisture and prevent weeds.” (From Native Seeds webpage)

Questions: How does growing tall like corn help you as you get older? What parts of your body help you to stand and walk and keep you stable like beans do in helping to keep roots stable? What parts of your body is used to protect you from the sun or other objects like leaves of squash plants?

Example 2.3.6. Standard 1-LS1-2.

Description: Read texts and use media to determine patterns in behavior of parents and offspring that help offspring survive.

Suggested classroom activities: Describe how the concept of the “Three Sisters” has been passed down from one generation to another in Native American communities. This knowledge has been passed down from grandparents and parents to children to help them to survive through knowing how to grow these crops together and they are called “sisters” because family help each other to survive.

Questions: How do you and your family help each other? How do your parents and grandparents help you? How will/do you help your younger siblings or children when you grow older?

Example 2.3.7. Standard 1-LS3-1.

Description: Make observations to construct an evidence-based account that young plants and animals are like, but not exactly like, their parents.

Take home exercise: Ask students to describe how they are alike and different from their brothers/sisters/cousins. Ask students to ask other family members how they are alike or different from their siblings/cousins.

Context 3: Drums.

Drums are used during feast day dances and other ceremonies. Students may hear and see different types of drums at pow-wows or cultural ceremonies at other Indigenous communities. Students can reflect on their experiences listening to and hearing drums when learning for the following standards.

Example 2.3.8. Standard 1-PS4-1.

Description: Plan and conduct investigations to provide evidence that vibrating materials can make sound and that sound can make materials vibrate.

Suggested classroom activities: Questions: What is needed for a drum to make sound? Is the drum louder when you beat it harder or softer with a drumstick? Do you feel vibrations when you are close to someone singing with a drum?

Example 2.3.9. Standard 1-PS4-4.

Description: Use tools and materials to design and build a device that uses light or sound to solve the problem of communicating over a distance

Suggested classroom activities: Questions: What types or size of drums are better to use in a small room where people are listening close by? What types or size of drums are better to use outside for people who are further away to listen to?

Subsection 2.3.2 Second Grade

Context 1: Sacred Mountains: importance of mountains to life in NM.

Mountains are sacred to Indigenous peoples around the world and in NM. Local mountains can provide a place-based setting to make geological, ecological, and cultural connections that help students learn the following NM STEM Ready! Science standards.

Example 2.3.10. Standard 2-ESS1-1.

Description: Use information from several sources to provide evidence that Earth events can occur quickly or slowly.

Take home exercise: Ask students to ask a family member about the origin story and name in their Native language of a mountain sacred to their Pueblo/community. Have students research how long it took the mountain to form from a geologic perspective.

Example 2.3.11. Standard 2-ESS2-1.

Description: Compare multiple solutions designed to slow or prevent wind or water from changing the shape of the land.

Questions: What parts of a mountain does water flow faster (steep hillslopes, flat areas or lakes/reservoirs, near the top of the mountain or bottom of the mountain)? What parts of the mountain are very windy? How does water change the land on a mountain? How does wind change the land or vegetation on a mountain?

Example 2.3.12. Standard 2-ESS2-2.

Description: Develop a model to represent the shapes and kinds of land and bodies of water in an area.

Have students draw or model their sacred mountain or another nearby mountain. Questions: Where is snow usually located on the mountain in the winter? Where are the streams, springs, and lakes/reservoirs?

Take home exercise: Ask students to as a family or community member the names of nearby water bodies located on the sacred mountain in their Native language.

Example 2.3.13. Standard 2-LS4-1.

Description: Make observations of plants and animals to compare the diversity of life in different habitats.

Questions: What types of plants and animals do you see near your home or village? What different types of plants and animals do you see in the mountains?

Example 2.3.14. Standard 2-LS4-1.

Description: Make observations of plants and animals to compare the diversity of life in different habitats.

Questions: What types of plants and animals do you see near your home or village? What different types of plants and animals do you see in the mountains?

Example 2.3.15. Standard 2-LS2-1.

Description: Plan and conduct an investigation to determine if plants need sunlight and water to grow.

Explain how New Mexico has a lot of sunlight and plants in NM are mostly limited by water in order to grow. Important sources of water in NM are large rivers like the Rio Grande and the mountains because mountains receive a lot of precipitation.

Questions: Where in NM do we see a lot of plants/vegetation growing? Why do we see more trees and grasses growing close to the Rio Grande (in the Bosque) compared to other areas? Why do we see more forests in the mountains in NM compared to other areas?

Example 2.3.16. Standard 2-LS2-2.

Description: Develop a simple model that mimics the function of an animal in dispersing seeds or pollinating plants

In-class exercises Ask students about what animals live in the mountains. Explain to students how some animals like mountain lions or bears can travel long distances and seeds that they eat or are caught in their fur can be spread across those distances.

Look up videos of animals caught on trail cameras by the Pueblo of Santa Ana Natural Resource Department on their Facebook page. Ask students to think about how each of the different types of animals might spread seeds.

Example 2.3.17. Standard 2-PS1-1.

Description: Plan and conduct an investigation to describe and classify different kinds of materials by their observable properties.

Take home exerfcise: Have students collect different pieces of object found in nature like twigs, pieces of wood, rocks, and leaves. Ask students to get a cup of water or find some standing water and to drop each object in the water. Explain to students that mountain streams can flow from the top of the mountains to the bottom of mountains and some streams connect to the Rio Grande.

Questions: Which of the natural objects that you collected and dropped in the water is most likely to travel the furthest in a mountain stream?

Example 2.3.18. Standard 2-PS1-2.

Description: Analyze data obtained from testing different materials to determine which materials have the properties that are best suited for an intended purpose.

Take home exercise: Have students collect different pieces of object found in nature like twigs, pieces of wood, rocks, and leaves. Ask students to get a cup of water or find some standing water and to drop each object in the water. Explain to students that mountain streams can flow from the top of the mountains to the bottom of mountains and some streams connect to the Rio Grande.

Questions: Which of the natural objects that you collected and dropped in the water is most likely to travel the furthest in a mountain stream?

Subsection 2.3.3 Third Grade

Context 1: Our home, environment, and living beings.

This context refers to the homes, environment, plants and animals in NM. Using NM as a broader place-based settings will help make concepts learned in the following standards more relevant to students.

Example 2.3.19. Standard 3-ESS2-1.

Description: Represent data in tables and graphical displays to describe typical weather conditions expected during a particular season.

In-class exercise: Go to the Weather Spark website https://weatherspark.com/. Type in “Bernalillo” in the search box and click on “Bernalillo, New Mexico” from the drop down box. View the graphics with the students. Refer to the “Pacing Guide for Teachers of Pueblo Students” and ask students to relate the highs and lows of temperature, precipitation, rainfall, daylight, and cloud cover to the different community activities listed in the Pacing Guide.

Example 2.3.20. Standard 3-ESS2-2.

Description: Obtain and combine information to describe climates in different regions of the world.

In-class exercise: Go to the Weather Spark website https://weatherspark.com/. Click on the different cities from around the world and compare the weather/climate information to Bernalillo, New Mexico.

Example 2.3.21. Standard 3-ESS3-1.

Description: Make a claim about the merit of a design solution that reduces the impacts of a weather-related hazard.

In-class exercise: Pick a city that you looked up in the in-class exercise for 3-ESS2-2 that has a different climate than Bernalillo. Look up the type of homes that are built in that city. Compare those homes to adobe houses found in communities in NM. Discuss with students why adobe houses may or may not work better in the city with a different climate than the houses that are found there.

Example 2.3.22. Standard 3-LS1-1.

Description: Develop models to describe that organisms have unique and diverse life cycles but all have in common birth, growth, reproduction, and death.

In-class exercise: Ask students to make a drawing of a typical crop grown in their community. Ask them to draw how the plant looks during the different seasons: winter, spring, summer, fall. Ask students to ask a family or community member how to describe the different seasons in their Native language.

Example 2.3.23. Standard 3-LS2-1.

Description: Construct an argument that some animals form groups that help members survive.

Questions What are some animals in NM that you see in groups? How do deer, elk, wolves, and coyotes living in groups help them survive?

Example 2.3.24. Standard 3-LS3-1.

Description: Analyze and interpret data to provide evidence that plants and animals have traits inherited from parents and that variation of these traits exists in a group of similar organisms.

Questions What are some traits of deer and elk? What traits are similar and what traits are different between deer and elk?

Example 2.3.25. Standard 3-LS3-2.

Description: Use evidence to support the explanation that traits can be influenced by the environment.

In-class exercise: Explain to students how the color and thickness of the coats of elk can change to adapt to the environment. Discuss with students how the coat of an elk in NM might be different than the coat of an elk in a colder, less sunny location.

Example 2.3.26. Standard 3-LS4-1.

Description: Analyze and interpret data from fossils to provide evidence of the organisms and the environments in which they lived long ago.

External resources: NM Museum of Natural History and Science “Brief Guide to Fossils” Brief Guide to Fossils. NM Museum of Natural History and Science “Dinosaurs, Ancient Reptiles, and Paleontology” coloring sheets. ColoringSheets

Example 2.3.27. Standard 3-LS4-2.

Description: Use evidence to construct an explanation for how the variations in characteristics among individuals of the same species may provide advantages in surviving, finding mates, and reproducing.

Questions: What are some characteristics of the roadrunner? How might some of these characteristics help a roadrunner be better at surviving and reproducing more than other roadrunners?

Example 2.3.28. Standard 3-LS4-3.

Description: Construct an argument with evidence that in a particular habitat some organisms can survive well, some survive less well, and some cannot survive at all.

In-class exercise: Explain to students the difference between a native species and an invasive species. Have students look up the differences between a cottonwood tree, a native species to NM, and a Russian olive, an invasive species to NM. Discuss with students the differences and similarities between how cottonwoods and Russian olive trees survive along the Rio Grande.

Example 2.3.29. Standard 3-LS4-4.

Description: Make a claim about the merit of a solution to a problem caused when the environment changes and the types of plants and animals that live there may change.

In-class exercise: In-class exercise: Have a discussion with students about the Rio Grande silvery minnow and different strategies used to protect the silvery minnow SilveryMinnow.

Take home exercise: Ask students to ask an elder in their community about how their community interacts or used to interact with the silvery minnow and what the silvery minnow is called in their Native language.

Context 2: Hiking and Fishing.

Hiking and fishing is a common practice in NM. In this context, students can relate activities done while hiking and fishing to concepts addressed in the following standards.

Example 2.3.30. Standard 3-PS2-1.

Description: Plan and conduct an investigation to provide evidence of the effects of balanced and unbalanced forces on the motion of an object.

Questions: When casting a fishing line, how are forces on the fishing line balanced or unbalanced? When reeling in a fish caught in your line, are the forces balanced or unbalanced?

Example 2.3.31. Standard 3-PS2-2.

Description: Make observations and/or measurements of an object’s motion to provide evidence that a pattern can be used to predict future motion.

Questions: What happens when you go fishing on a windy day compared to a day that isn’t windy? If it is a windy day while fishing, how do you adjust how you cast your line?

Example 2.3.32. Standard 3-PS2-3.

Description: Ask questions to determine cause and effect relationships of electric or magnetic interactions between two objects not in contact with each other.

In-Class exercise: Explain to students how using a compass and map can be helpful when hiking to make sure you don’t get lost. Explain how compasses use the magnetic attraction between a needle and the North direction to help people know their direction. Create a scavenger hunt for students to find objects using a compass.

External Resource: "How to Teach Students to Use a Compass"- https://sciencing.com/how-to-teach-kids-about-longitude-and-latitude-12749932.html

Subsection 2.3.4 Fourth Grade

Context 1: The Rio Grande Basin.

This context refers to the homes, environment, plants and animals in NM. Using NM as a broader place-based settings will help make concepts learned in the following standards more relevant to students.

Example 2.3.33. Standard 4-ESS1-1.

Description: Identify evidence from patterns in rock formations and fossils in rock layers to support an explanation for changes in a landscape over time.

In-class exercise: Refer to Reano and Ridgway (2015) 28 , show Figure 1, and discuss with students how different rock layers can be tied to Native American culture, as discussed in the article. You can discuss how these rock layers are located in other areas of the Rio Grande Basin and other tribes and Pueblos may have similar connections.

Example 2.3.34. Standard 4-ESS2-1.

Description: Make observations and/or measurements to provide evidence of the effects of weathering or the rate of erosion by water, ice, wind, or vegetation.

In-class exercise: Make connections of weathering and erosion to the formation of the Great Sand Dunes National Park (https://www.doi.gov/blog/12-things-you-didnt-know-about-great-sand-dunes-national-park-and-preserve) and it's tie to the Rio Grande River (https://drive.google.com/file/d/18izjh6OZT3omW7jDUz4OxGWCZ7engdmo/view?usp=sharing).

Example 2.3.35. Standard 4-ESS2-2.

Description: Analyze and interpret data from maps to describe patterns of Earth’s features.

In-class or take home exercise: Ask students to explore features of the Rio Grande Basin using Google Earth (https://earth.google.com/web/).

Example 2.3.36. Standard 4-ESS3-1.

Description: Obtain and combine information to describe that energy and fuels are derived from natural resources and their uses affect the environment.

In-class exercise: Discuss with students the impacts of oil and gas drilling in the Chaco Canyon region (https://www.wilderness.org/wild-places/new-mexico/oil-and-gas-drilling-chaco-canyon).

Example 2.3.37. Standard 4-ESS3-2.

Description: Generate and compare multiple solutions to reduce the impacts of natural Earth processes on humans.

In-class exercise: Discuss with students the natural occurrence of fire and fire history in the Rio Grande Bosque (https://nmnaturalhistory.org/bosque-education-guide/chapter-6-fire-rio-grande-bosque).

Talk with students about Indigenous burning practices: (https://www.nature.org/en-us/membership-and-giving/donate-to-our-mission/gift-and-estate-planning/newsletter/forever-forests/).

Example 2.3.38. Standard 4-LS1-1.

Description: Construct an argument that plants and animals have internal and external structures that function to support survival, growth, behavior, and reproduction.

In-class exercise: Explain to students the features of the Rio Grande cottonwood tree that help it to survive, grow, and reproduce along the Rio Grande bosque. Talk with students about how changes made by humans have impacted cottonwood growth along the Rio Grande (https://nmnaturalhistory.org/bosque-education-guide/chapter-2-bosque-background).

Take home exercise: Ask students to ask a family or community member what the name of the cottonwood is in their Native language. Ask students to talk with elders about changes they have noticed to the Rio Grande bosque.

Example 2.3.39. Standard 4-LS1-2.

Description: Use a model to describe that animals receive different types of information through their senses, process the information in their brain, and respond to the information in different ways.

In-class exercise: Introduce students to the Native frogs and toads to the Albuquerque/Bernalillo bosque area (https://www.cabq.gov/artsculture/biopark/documents/Native%20Amphibians%20Brochure.pdf). Talk with students about how frogs and toads are seen and heard more after it rains and ask them to think about how these amphibians detect rain.

Explain how some toads can detect rain vibrations (https://www.cell.com/current-biology/pdf/S0960-9822(16)31330-6.pdf)

Take home exercise: Ask students to ask a family or community member how to describe frogs or toads in their Native language.

Example 2.3.40. Standard 4-PS4-2.

Description: Develop a model to describe that light reflecting from objects and entering the eye allows objects to be seen.

In-class exercise: Talk with students about the different ski basins in the mountains in the headwaters of the Rio Grande Basin. Explain to students that skiers need ski goggles because snow reflects sunlight into skiers eyes and goggles are needed to protect the eyes.

Introduce students to goggles made and used by Indigenous peoples in Alaska to protect their eyes from sunlight reflecting off of snow (https://www.smithsonianmag.com/smithsonian-institution/snow-goggles-demonstrate-indigenous-ingenuity-180973738/).

Subsection 2.3.5 Fifth Grade

Context 1: Solar energy and soils, elements needed to grow our food.

In this context, students will relate to how Indigenous peoples connect to the sun and use solar energy, including through renewable energy technology like photovoltaics. Students can relate science standards to the need for healthy soils for farming.

Example 2.3.47. Standard 5-ESS1-1.

Description: Support an argument that differences in the apparent brightness of the sun compared to other stars is due to their relative distances from the Earth.

In-class or take home exercise:Introduce students to photovoltaics by discussing how some Native communities in the Southwest rely on solar energy technology to provide electricity in their homes. Show students the Native Renewables website (https://www.nativerenewables.org/). Download and pass out the zine created by Native Renewables that explains photovoltaics (https://static1.squarespace.com/static/5f491be9a82eed6a01fd24ac/t/6018a18e281db443ae79bb0e/1612226961043/Solar%2BMinizine%2Bv14.pdf).

Questions:What are ways you use electricity in your homes? What happens when your home doesn’t have electricity? Why do photovoltaics only work during the day? Can photovoltaics work at night using light from other stars?

Example 2.3.48. Standard 5-ESS1-2.

Description: Represent data in graphical displays to reveal patterns of daily changes in length and direction of shadows, day and night, and the seasonal appearance of some stars in the night sky.

Take home exercise: Introduce students to passive solar energy by handing out this handout on passive solar home design in NM (https://www.nmsolar.org/wp-content/uploads/2017/04/Passive-Solar-Design.pdf).

Ask students to walk around their home, village, and neighborhoods to identify how some homes make use of passive solar energy.

Example 2.3.49. Standard 5-ESS2-1.

Description: Develop a model using an example to describe ways the geosphere, biosphere, hydrosphere, and/or atmosphere interacts.

Questions: How do you interact with each of the spheres? What sphere is your home built on and the materials used for your home built on? What sphere does your food come from? How does the sun interact with each sphere?

Example 2.3.50. Standard 5-ESS2-2.

Description: Describe and graph the amounts and percentages of water and fresh water in various reservoirs to provide evidence about the distribution of water on Earth.

In-class exercise: Discuss with students how water is distributed in New Mexico. Refer to the FAQs about water page of the NM Bureau of Geology and Mineral Resources: https://geoinfo.nmt.edu/faq/water/home.html). Relate to the global distribution of rainfall (https://www.climate-charts.com/World-Climate-Maps.html) and discuss how NM is in an arid climate and we get a lot of solar radiation that easily evaporates our rainfall. Thus, we have less surface water and rely more on groundwater compared to other areas in the world.

Example 2.3.51. Standard 5-ESS3-1.

Description: Obtain and combine information about ways individual communities use science ideas to protect the Earth’s resources and environment.

Take home or in-class exercise: Introduce students to the NM Tribal Resilience Action Network by showing this YouTube video that discusses how Pueblos and Tribes in NM are addressing climate change:

Also, refer to the Eight Northern Indian Pueblos, Inc.-Office of Environmental Technical Assistance website (http://enipc-oeta.org/home.htm) to discuss how Pueblos are addressing other environmental issues.

Example 2.3.52. Standard 5-LS1-1.

Description: Support an argument that plants get the materials they need for growth chiefly from air and water.

Take home or in-class exercise: Invite a Pueblo farmer to talk to students about Pueblo farming practices or ask students to talk to a family or community member about how the work they do in their fields to help their crops grow.

Example 2.3.53. Standard 5-LS2-1.

Description: Develop a model to describe the movement of matter among plants, animals, decomposers, and the environment.

Example 2.3.102. Standard MS-ESS3-3.

Description: Apply scientific principles to design a method for monitoring and minimizing a human impact on the environment.

In-class exercise: Talk with students about how fire suppression by humans in the last century has led to a build up of fuels in forests leading to more larger and severe wildfires. Discuss how Indigenous peoples are revitalizing cultural burning practices which was used prior to the fire suppression era to reduce the build up of fuels (https://www.nature.org/en-us/magazine/magazine-articles/indigenous-controlled-burns-california/ and https://www.nature.org/en-us/membership-and-giving/donate-to-our-mission/gift-and-estate-planning/newsletter/forever-forests/).

Questions:

How has fire suppression by humans impacted the environment? What are ways we can mitigate impacts of large wildfires without further fire suppression?
