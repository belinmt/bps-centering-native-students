Section 2.2 Culturally Responsive Science Framework

The purpose of this framework is to provide additional detail for developing a culturally responsive science curriculum. This supplementary framework should be used in addition to the “Culturally Responsive Curriculum Framework: Indigenous Communities and Pueblo Nations Served by Bernalillo Public Schools”. This section will include:

  • Core Values in Practice

  • Examples inside and outside Bernalillo Public Schools

  • Prevalent Symbols and Impacts of Colonization

  • Centering Students

  • Desired Outcome

Subsection 2.2.1 Core Values in Practice

  • Be Purposefully Culturally Responsive

  • Nurture Knowledge and Be Responsibly Committed to Long-Term Pathways

  • Be Community Centered in Educational Approaches

  • Be Accountable By Honoring Students, Their Histories, and The District Vision

  • Honor Identity and Be Caring

  • Acknowledge Perseverance Through Mutual Respect

  • Strive for Innovation and Creativity

  • Show Pride Through Collaboration

Subsection 2.2.2 Examples inside and outside Bernalillo Public Schools

  • Keres Classes (TEK embedded in Indigenous languages) 2 

  • Santa Fe Indian School Agriscience Program 3 

  • Dream Dine Charter School experiential curriculum 4 

  • Indian Pueblo Cultural Center Indigenous Wisdom Curriculum 5 

  • Pueblo Liasons

  • American Indian Science and Engineering Society (AISES) Pre-College Programs 6 

  • Native American Community Academy 7  School-based mentoring program and College Enrichment Program

  • SFIS Community Based Education 8 

Subsection 2.2.3 Prevalent Symbols and Impacts of Colonization

  • Lack of inclusion of local/place-based knowledge and socio-cultural connections in western science 9 

  • Ethical concerns of western science research 10 

  • Low representation of Native Americans is STEM fields 11 

  • Traditional Western Science training teaches students to be disconnected from communities

  • Policy decisions that have led to environmental injustices and disproportionate negative impacts on Indigenous communities

Subsection 2.2.4 Centering Students

  • Teach science concepts in place-based contexts

  • Support students in learning terminology in their Native languages and relating to traditional stories

  • Teach students about STEM-related issues that impact Indigenous peoples like climate change, energy development, genetics research (re: claims to Indigeneity), endangered species

  • Teach students history of science and that there are multiple ways of knowing but Western Science has dominated education

  • Expand the definition of “science” in classrooms to show how daily activities of students are ways that students practice science

  • Use examples of how Indigenous knowledge and TEK have affirmed or added to larger body of science knowledge

  • Point to science that has been done by Indigenous scientists

  • Hands on lessons

  • Collaborate with local tribal environmental and natural resources departments

Subsection 2.2.5 Desired Outcomes

  • Students will understand science lessons and concepts as not being abstract but a part of their communities and everyday lives

  • Students will understand how science can be used to address issues facing their families and communities

  • Students will understand the lack of inclusion of Indigenous knowledge/voices in dominant STEM practice and education is a result of the Western science framework and colonial practices

  • Students will better understand how to see themselves as scientists and their potential to succeed in science
